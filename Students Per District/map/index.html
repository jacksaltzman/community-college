Community College Commuting Analysis

March 2026

This analysis maps the commuting patterns of 9M community college students across 1,219 campuses and all 435 congressional districts. It answers three questions: 1) How far do CC students actually travel? 2) How many congressional districts does a single campus’ commute zone touch? 3) And what does that mean for how we build coalitions at Accountable?

TL;DR: CC students are local, but “local” and “concentrated in one district” are not the same thing. In dense urban areas, a 15-mile commute radius can split across 5+ districts. In rural areas, a much wider commute radius often sits neatly inside a single district. Neither extreme gives us everything we need. The opportunity lives somewhere in the middle.

1. CC Students Are Local, And That’s The Point

Community college students don’t travel far to get to school. That’s by design. The question for us isn’t whether CC students are local, but how that locality translates to political geography.

Here’s the commuting data, broken out by the type of area a campus sits in.

The headline: the median CC student lives 10 miles from campus. At the 75th percentile, it’s 22 miles. Even in rural areas, where students commute furthest, half are still within 27 miles. These students are, by any reasonable definition, local.

But a 15-mile radius around a campus creates a circle of 707 square miles. In dense urban areas, 73% of congressional districts are smaller than that circle. The students are local; the district boundaries do not contain them.

2. The District Geography Problem

Congressional districts are drawn to contain equal populations (~760,000 people). This means dense areas get geographically tiny districts, while rural areas get enormous ones. A 15-mile commute radius captures a fundamentally different share of a district depending on where you draw the circle. The data confirms this pattern across all 1,219 campuses in the dataset:

Read this table left to right: as you move from urban to rural, enrollment drops, the number of districts touched drops, and commute radius grows. Both ends of this spectrum are problematic for Accountable.

3. The Sweet Spot

The right targeting question isn’t “which CCs are biggest?” It’s “which districts accumulate the most CC students from campuses that don’t bleed everywhere?”

Reframing the unit of analysis from campus to district surfaces a middle ground where enrollment, concentration, and district boundaries align. 130 campuses have both 10,000+ enrollment and reach 3 or fewer districts. Together, they account for 2.3M students, 25% of CC enrollment nationwide. by filtering enrollment and districts reached.

These campuses are enough to generate coalition-scale enrollment, but sit in geographies where commute zones don’t fragment across a dozen districts. Many are midsize city campuses or large city campuses that happen to sit in districts with favorable geography.

4. What This Means for Accountable

This analysis has three direct implications for how we think about GTM.

  1. The unit of targeting should be the district, not the campus.
  2. Urban mega-campuses are awareness plays, not coalition plays.
  3. The sweet spot is a real, quantifiable segment.

The full dataset covers 9M students across all congressional districts. 281 districts have 10k+ students. 7 swing states represent 1.35M students across 216 campuses. The community college population is large and ubiquitous.

5. But Concentration Alone Isn’t a Strategy

Everything above is a spatial analysis. It does not tell us whether CC students are the right population for Accountable to build around. A 30-year longitudinal study using Monitoring the Future data found that high schoolers planning to attend CCs had half the odds of engaging in conventional civic activities compared to four-year-bound peers1. Separately, four-year students reported higher volunteering, voter registration, and news consumption than two-year attendees2. Community colleges also have a 55% first-year retention rate, meaning nearly half the user base disappears annually before we account for product churn3.

Of course, these aren’t disqualifying facts. They’re open questions. We don’t know whether CC students would engage with a civic engagement product like ours because nobody has offered them one. It’s our job to find out while simultaneously evaluating other high-density personas.

Methodology Note

This analysis combines three sources: 1) NCES PowerStats for student-level commuting distances by locale type, 2) IPEDS for campus-level enrollment, and 3) Census Bureau 119th Congress congressional district shapefiles intersected with NCES EDGE campus geocodes to compute district overlaps.

Commute zones are modeled as circular buffers around each campus at the 75th percentile commuting distance from that campus’s NCES locale code. This is a simplification but it captures the relevant scale.

Step 1: Identify Community Colleges

Campuses are selected from HD2023 using three inclusion groups:

GroupFilterCountDescription
1SECTOR = 4~880Traditional public two-year colleges
2SECTOR = 5~135Private not-for-profit two-year colleges
3SECTOR = 1 and HLOFFER = 5~245Public institutions whose highest offering is a bachelor’s degree — these are community colleges that were reclassified after their states authorized them to grant bachelor’s degrees (e.g., Florida, Colorado, Arizona, Washington)

Campuses with missing or zero coordinates are dropped. A post-processing step removes 37 additional institutions that were reclassified between HD2023 and HD2024 or whose IPEDS IDs became defunct due to mergers (primarily 11 Connecticut community colleges that consolidated into CT State Community College).

Final campus count: 1,219.

Step 2: Join Enrollment Data

Total 12-month unduplicated headcount enrollment is joined from EFFY2023, filtered to EFFYLEV = 1 (all students) and LSTUDY = 999 (total across all levels of study). This is a left join — campuses without enrollment data retain null values rather than being dropped.

Step 3: Classify Campus by Locale and Assign Commute Radius

Each campus is assigned a commute-shed radius based on its NCES locale code (the LOCALE field in HD2023). Radii come from the 75th-percentile (P75) student-to-campus crow-flies distance computed from the 2020 National Postsecondary Student Aid Study (NPSAS:20) via NCES PowerStats (retrieval code: swopse). The P75 was chosen over the median because it captures the commuting range of ~75% of students, reflecting the broader service area relevant to legislative outreach.

Step 4: Build Commute-Shed Circles

A circular buffer is drawn around each campus point using its assigned radius. To ensure accurate area calculations, campuses are grouped by region and projected into an appropriate equal-area coordinate reference system before buffering:

RegionCRSEPSG
Continental U.S.Albers Equal-Area Conic5070
AlaskaAlaska Albers3338
HawaiiHawaii Albers Equal-Area Conic102007

The resulting circles represent the estimated geographic area from which a campus draws commuting students.

Step 5: Intersect Circles with Congressional Districts

Each commute-shed circle is intersected with the 118th Congress congressional district boundaries. For every circle-district pair that overlaps:

  1. The exact geometric intersection is computed (not a bounding-box approximation)
  2. The overlap area is measured in square miles
  3. The fractional overlap is calculated as: overlap area / total district area

Pairs with fractional overlap below 1% are dropped, except that every campus retains at least its single best-overlapping district (this handles campuses in large at-large districts like Alaska, Montana, and Wyoming).

The district boundary files are cartographic (coast-clipped), so circles over water produce no overlap — this is expected and correct for coastal campuses.

Step 6: Identify the Primary District

For each campus, the district with the highest fractional overlap is flagged as the primary district. This is the district whose geographic area is most covered by the campus’s commute shed — not necessarily the district where the campus is physically located (though it usually is).

Key Assumptions

  1. Commute sheds are circular. In practice, commuting patterns follow road networks and natural geography. Circles are a simplification that works well for nationwide analysis but overestimates reach in areas with barriers (mountains, rivers, limited road access).
  2. NPSAS:20 P75 distance determines radius. Each campus’s commute radius is set to the 75th-percentile student-to-campus distance for its NCES locale code, as computed from NPSAS:20. Urban campuses get smaller circles because students commute shorter distances in dense areas. The 12 locale-specific radii range from 15 miles (large cities) to 58 miles (remote towns).
  3. Fractional overlap is the right metric. A campus’s influence on a district is measured by what fraction of the district’s land area falls within the commute shed. This means a campus in a small urban district may “cover” 30% of it, while the same campus covers only 0.5% of an adjacent rural district — reflecting the reality that community college students represent a larger share of the electorate in compact districts.
  4. Coast-clipped boundaries are correct. The cartographic boundary files exclude water, so a campus on the coast will have a commute circle that partially covers ocean. This water area produces no district overlap, which is the correct behavior — students don’t commute across water.
